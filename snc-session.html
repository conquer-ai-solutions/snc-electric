02
Probe
Surgical questions. Follow what they said, not the sequence.
These are deployed responsively — when the context earns them. Don't run through them like a checklist. Q6 is the most important: it's the anchor for Beat 5.
10–20 min · One thread at a time · Follow the energy in the room
Q1 — Quote-to-contract
Walk me through what happens from the moment a customer sends in a spec. Who touches it, where does it go, and where does it fall down?
Gets the actual process on the table — not the idealised version. You need to know who reads the documents today, in what order, and where the gaps are.
Listen for: a specific person's name, a specific moment of manual work, the phrase "it depends" — that's where the AI opportunity lives.
Q2 — Quote-to-contract
When a customer sends in non-standard terms — an unusual LD clause, a warranty requirement you don't normally accept — how does that get caught? Who catches it?
Surfaces the commercial risk in the current process and names the person bearing it. That person is your champion for the solution.
Listen for: "usually someone catches it" — that's the gap. "It's sometimes caught after we've already quoted" — that's the fire.
Q3 — ETO / design
You mentioned two engineers given the same spec come back with two different designs. Is there a documented "preferred" universe of designs — or is it tribal knowledge?
Establishes whether there's a baseline to learn from or whether we'd need to build the standard first. The answer also surfaces whether this is a process problem or a data problem.
Listen for: "some of it is written down" — that's the starting point. "It lives in people's heads" — that changes the scope entirely.
Q4 — BOM / upstream
When inventory or BOM errors surface — can you give me a concrete example of the last time it burned you? What was the upstream cause?
Grounds the abstract ERP/BOM problem in a real event. Real examples always reveal the actual problem, not the stated one. Used at Goodman — changed the entire scope.
Listen for: a drawing revision, a supplier change, a missed SKU — those are language problems upstream, not data problems downstream.
Q5 — Technology
Beyond Salesforce and M365 — what does the technology landscape look like around the commercial process? Has anything AI-adjacent been tried already?
Surfaces the integration surface and any existing AI investments. Ernst will engage most here. Also reveals whether Copilot is doing anything useful or just sitting there.
Listen for: "Copilot is deployed but nobody uses it for this" — that's a buying signal. "We tried X and it didn't work" — that's scope intelligence.
Q6 — Close beat · most important
What would have to be true for you to say, six months from now, that this was one of the best decisions you made this year?
Sets success criteria in their words. Whatever they say here is what you validate against in Beat 5. It also reveals whether this is a strategic priority or a tactical fix.
Listen for: emotional language alongside metrics. "My team stops dreading Mondays" is more useful than "20% efficiency gain" — it tells you what actually matters.

03
Synthesis
Name what they just described — in Conquer's language.
These concepts are mirrors, not openers. Deploy each one only when S&C has already described their own version of it. "What you're describing is exactly..." is the bridge. Don't deploy what hasn't been earned.
8–12 min · Responsive framing · Only deploy earned concepts
Quote-to-contract
Unstructured data → structured commercial intelligence
"What you're describing is exactly..."
Every inbound RFQ, spec, and PO is unstructured data. Someone on your team is doing a transformation — reading a 40-page document and turning it into a handful of structured decisions. That transformation is what AI does well, consistently, at scale, before anyone opens their inbox.
Only deploy if: they've described the manual reading process, mentioned document variety, or referenced humans in the loop between customer ask and quote.
Quote-to-contract
Proactive flag before the commitment is made
"The pattern we see is..."
The commercial risk in your process isn't that no one catches non-standard terms — it's that they're caught too late. By the time a flag surfaces, a quote is already in the customer's hands or a commitment has been made. The value isn't in catching more; it's in catching earlier.
Only deploy if: they've described T&C issues being caught downstream, or referenced the cost of responding before commercial review is complete.
ETO / design
Narrowing the solution space — not generating solutions
"The way we'd think about that is..."
The ETO problem isn't about generating the right design — it's about eliminating the wrong ones. There are billions of valid configurations, but a much smaller universe of preferred ones. AI's job isn't to design the product; it's to keep engineers inside the preferred universe and flag when they're drifting outside it.
Only deploy if: Anders S or Joseph has described the two-engineers-same-spec problem, or the "standardised inside, customised outside" framing has come up.
BOM / upstream
The visible problem is financial — the cause is a language problem
"What you're seeing in the ERP is..."
Mismatched inventory and BOM errors don't start as data problems — they start as language problems. A drawing revision that didn't propagate, a supplier change that wasn't captured, a spec that was interpreted differently. The downstream symptom is numerical, but the upstream cause is a document that didn't get read correctly, or at all.
Only deploy if: Anders H has described the BOM/ERP problem and connected it to upstream process issues rather than pure data quality.
Universal
The staircase — own our destiny first, integrate later
"The way we'd want to approach this..."
The fastest path to working software isn't starting in your environment — it's starting in ours, with your data and your documents, and proving the extraction works before we touch your DevOps team. Phase 1 lives in Conquer's cloud. Phase 2 moves it into yours. That's how we ship in three months instead of six.
Only deploy if: Ernst has asked about integration complexity, or the conversation has moved toward scoping and timeline.
Universal
Start narrow, ship fast, earn the right to expand
"The pattern that works is..."
Every engagement that works starts with a single, bounded, high-frequency problem. Not ten things — one. Solve it completely. Ship it. Get S&C seeing the benefit. Then the second conversation happens naturally. The organisations that try to boil the ocean first are still in discovery a year later.
Only deploy if: the conversation is expanding into too many use cases at once, or someone is asking how all three problems get solved simultaneously.
