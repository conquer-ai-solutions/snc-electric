Use Cases

What they actually care about

Three distinct problems surfaced in the intro call. One is the clear entry point. The other two are real but need more discovery before we can land them.

Priority · Use Case 01
Quote-to-Contract — Upstream Commercial Process

Joseph named this explicitly as the best technology–problem fit on the January call. S&C put a human (or set of humans) between the customer ask and the purchase order. Customers send in specs and terms and conditions through Salesforce — huge variety, lots of manual review, lots of gap risk.

The opportunity: T&C review against spec. Classifying and extracting from inbound commercial documents. Flagging non-standard terms. Generating draft responses. All of it running through their existing M365 / Salesforce stack. This is close to what we showed them in the FI demo and it landed.

The analogue: Bolaji will reference a prior Conquer POC built for a greenhouse technology manufacturer — a company that also receives highly varied inbound commercial documents and needs to extract, validate, and match them against internal standards. Same pattern, different industry. Bolaji to present this unbranded as a worked example of what the solution could look like for S&C.

Use Case 02
ETO Configurator & Design Optimisation

Anders S's personal obsession. S&C build engineer-to-order switchgear with billions of variants. Two engineers given the same spec produce two completely different designs. Both valid. One preferred. No way to search prior designs. They start with a white piece of paper every time.

The angle: Not design generation — narrowing the solution space. Joseph reframed it well: validation of requirements against design. Can AI flag when a proposed design is outside S&C's preferred universe? That's the tractable version. We've done something similar (NDA-limited on the specifics).

Be honest: This is genuinely hard and we don't have a complete answer yet. The whiteboard is the right place to explore it — say that directly.

Use Case 03
Inventory / BOM / ERP Intelligence

Anders H's concern. Mismatched inventory tracing back to bill of materials errors. Oracle ERP. Millions of data points. SKU intelligence. The symptom is financial — a number in the wrong place on the balance sheet — but the root cause is upstream language problems: drawing revisions, supplier changes, unlinked process steps.

Our position: This is traditional AI / data science territory, not our core. We were honest about that on the intro call. Don't walk it back now. The positioning is: the visible problem is mathematical, but upstream language issues are causing it, and that's where we can help. Use Case 01 is the wedge into this longer-term.

"Where I think there's the clearest technology-plus-problem fit — it's the quote-to-contract cycle. Terms and conditions review versus spec review. There's so much variety in how that comes through Salesforce, with a lot of humans involved." — Joseph Matamoros, January 30 intro call
Whiteboard Playbook

How to run this session

90 minutes. Don't fill it. The best whiteboard sessions are the ones where the client talks more than us.

Don't re-pitch. They know who we are. A quick "thank you for making this happen, we know it took a while to find the slot" acknowledges the effort without dwelling on it. Then hand it to them: "We've done our homework on what you told us in January. Before we go through our thinking, has anything changed on your side since then?"

This does three things: it shows we listened, it gives them space to redirect if priorities have shifted, and it starts the session with them talking, not us.

These are the things we don't have enough of yet:

  • On quote-to-contract: Walk me through what happens from the moment a customer sends in a spec. Who touches it? Where does it go? Where do things fall down most often? What does "getting it wrong" actually look like?
  • On the configurator: What does "preferred design" mean in practice — is there a written standard somewhere, or is it tribal knowledge? How many people currently make that call?
  • On ERP / inventory: When you say the BOM is the upstream problem — can you give me a concrete example of the last time this burned you?
  • On technology: Beyond Salesforce and M365 — what does the tech landscape look like around the commercial process? Is there any internal AI already touching this?
  • On urgency: If this is still a priority, what would "good" look like by end of year?

Show: The prior greenhouse technology POC (unbranded, Bolaji's play). This is the closest analogue to the quote-to-contract problem — document ingestion, extraction, matching against a standard. Make it concrete, make it fast, don't over-explain it.

Don't show: The FI demo again unless they ask. We already showed it in January. Repeating it signals we haven't moved on from discovery.

Draw on the whiteboard: The before/after of their current quote-to-contract flow as we understand it. Then the version with AI in it. Let them correct us. The corrections are the signal we need.

The staircase framing always works well here. We need something that:

  • We can own our own destiny on (less dependency on their DevOps / internal teams)
  • Ships in 3 months or less
  • Has clear, measurable impact
  • Lives close to their existing process (Salesforce / M365)

Quote-to-contract checks all four boxes. The ETO configurator does not — it's a phase two or three conversation. Be explicit about that so expectations are right.

Phase Scope Dependency Timeline
Discovery Whiteboard → pre-proposal. Define the problem, scope the solution, agree on success metrics. None from their side Now → 2 weeks
Phase 1 Build in Conquer cloud. T&C / spec extraction and review agent. Mocked against their Salesforce endpoints. Prove the thing works. Sample docs + API schemas 6–8 weeks
Phase 2 Deploy into S&C's cloud environment. Live integration with Salesforce. Handover to their team. DevOps access + AWS/Azure 4–6 weeks

Don't leave the room without a defined next step. The close should be: "Here's what we heard today. We'll turn this into a pre-proposal within two weeks — a clear problem statement, a proposed solution, a timeline, and a cost. You tell us where we've misunderstood something, and we iterate from there. Does that work?"

If they're warm on quote-to-contract, ask directly who on their side would need to be involved in the pre-proposal review. Get names. That's the next relationship to open.

If Joseph or Ernst pushes on feasibility of the ETO configurator, don't shut it down — say you'd want to dig into it more in a follow-on session with the people closest to that process. Keep the door open.

Watch-Outs

Where this can go wrong

Things that killed momentum on other accounts — and the S&C-specific risks to manage.

Don't get dragged into the ERP rabbit hole Ernst or Anders H may try to pull us into the Oracle / inventory problem in detail. It's a real problem, but it's not our entry point. Acknowledge it, validate it, and redirect: "That's a really important problem — and our instinct is that the root cause is upstream in the language and process layer, not in the data. Let's make sure we nail the commercial process first, and then we'll have the credibility and the context to look at this properly."
Don't let the ETO configurator become the whole meeting Anders S is personally excited about this. It's genuinely interesting. But it's also genuinely complex and genuinely ambiguous — two engineers, same input, two outputs. That ambiguity is a red flag for fast delivery. Keep the conversation exploratory, not committal. "I want to spend more time on this with people who are close to the process — let's not rush it today."
Watch for scope creep before we've landed anything Five months to get this session. The pressure to close something is real. Don't over-commit to show progress. A tight, deliverable Phase 1 is worth more than a sprawling proposal we can't staff or ship.
Atlee is an asset, not a crutch She's in the room and she's trusted. But she's there to open doors, not to carry the session. If we lean on her too much, it signals we can't stand on our own. Use her for room-reading and relationship navigation, not for content.
The energy is good — don't over-manage it Anders S said "love the energy" at the end of the January call. The room likes us. The biggest risk is being too buttoned-up and killing the warmth that got us here. Be direct. Be honest when we don't know something. That's what landed the first call.

Bottom Line

What winning looks like

We leave the room with one agreed use case, two or three names for the next conversation, and a clear mandate to send a pre-proposal. That's it. Don't try to close more than that.

The metric of success

Anders said it himself in January: "our metric of success on this type of call is just that we don't scare you off enough that you wouldn't entertain the whiteboarding session." We're past that bar. Now the metric is: we leave with a pre-proposal mandate and a timeline. Everything else is a bonus.
